# Ionospheric Propagation

Ionospheric propagation (also known as **sky wave propagation**) refers to radio waves that travel high up into the Earth's upper atmosphere (the ionosphere) and are reflected back down to Earth, enabling long-distance communication.

## Basic Principle

The ionosphere is a layer of charged particles (ions and free electrons) in the Earth's upper atmosphere (approximately 60-1000 km above Earth's surface). When radio waves enter this region, they are **refracted** (bent) back toward Earth. This allows signals to travel beyond the line-of-sight horizon.

## Ionosphere Layers

The ionosphere consists of several layers:

- **D Layer** (60-90 km): Exists only during daylight; absorbs low-frequency radio waves
- **E Layer** (90-150 km): Also known as the Kennelly-Heaviside layer; reflects medium frequencies
- **F Layer** (150-500+ km): The most important layer for long-distance communication; splits into F1 (day) and F2 (always present) layers

## Frequency Range

- [Frequency_Range:: **3-30 MHz (HF Band)**]
- Below 3 MHz: Absorbed by D layer
- Above 30 MHz: Pass through ionosphere (used for space communications)

## Critical Parameters

- **Critical Frequency**: The maximum frequency that can be reflected back when transmitted vertically
- **Maximum Usable Frequency (MUF)**: Highest frequency that can be used for communication at a given distance
- **Virtual Height**: The apparent height from which the wave appears to reflect

## Applications

- [Applications:: **Long-distance radio communication**]
- [Applications:: **Amateur (Ham) Radio**]
- [Applications:: **International Broadcasting**]
- [Applications:: **Military Communication**]
- [Applications:: **Ship-to-Shore Communication**]

## Advantages

- [Advantages:: **Enables long-distance communication without relays**]
- [Advantages:: **Works beyond line-of-sight distance**]
- [Advantages:: **Cost-effective for intercontinental communication**]

## Limitations

- [Limitations:: **Affected by day/night cycles** - signal strength varies between day and night]
- [Limitations:: **Subject to solar activity** - sunspot cycles affect ionospheric conditions]
- [Limitations:: **Signal fading** - multipath propagation causes signal fluctuations]
- [Limitations:: **Limited bandwidth** - not suitable for high-data-rate applications]
- [Limitations:: **D layer absorption** - low frequencies absorbed during daylight]

## Depends On
- [Depends:: **Solar radiation**]
  Solar activity affects ionosphere layer density and electron concentration.
- [Depends:: **Time of day**]
  D layer disappears at night; E and F layers change.
- [Depends:: **Season**]
  Atmospheric conditions vary with seasons affecting propagation.
- [Depends:: **Frequency**]
  Different frequencies are reflected or absorbed by different layers.
- [Depends:: **Earth's magnetic field**]
  Affects the polarization and path of radio waves.

## Factors Affecting Ionospheric Propagation

1. **Solar Activity**: Sunspots and solar flares affect ionosphere density
2. **Time of Day**: D layer disappears at night, E/F layers change
3. **Season**: Atmospheric conditions vary with seasons
