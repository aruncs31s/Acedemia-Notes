# Interference and System Capacity

> [!summary]+ Summary
> 1. **Interference Types**:
>    - Co-channel interference: Cells using the same frequencies need spatial separation (distance D). Managed via reuse ratio Q = D/R = √3N - trade-off between capacity (smaller N) vs. quality (larger N)
>    - Adjacent channel interference: Leakage from nearby frequencies, causing the "near-far problem" where a strong nearby signal blocks weaker distant signals
> 2. **Trunking**: Pool-based channel allocation - channels are shared on demand among many users rather than permanently assigned. Traffic measured in Erlangs.
> 3. **Grade of Service (GoS)**: Probability a call is blocked/delayed during peak hours (e.g., 2% GoS = 2 blocked calls per 100 attempts). Used to design appropriate channel capacity.
> The key insight: System capacity is limited by three bottlenecks (radio, control link, switch) - improving just radio isn't enough if other components can't handle traffic


Interference is the major limiting factor in cellular system performance, causing background cross-talk on voice channels and missed or blocked calls on control channels. The overall system capacity is typically capped by three distinct bottlenecks: **radio capacity**, **control link capacity**, and **switch capacity**. Improving only the radio capacity is insufficient if the network switch or control links cannot handle the increased traffic.

## 1. Co-Channel Interference

This occurs when different, physically separated cells (known as co-channel cells) use the exact same set of frequencies.

- **Power Control is Ineffective**: Unlike standard background noise, co-channel interference cannot be combated by simply increasing the transmitter's carrier power, because doing so simultaneously increases the interference caused to neighboring co-channel cells.

- **Spatial Separation**: Instead, it is managed by physically separating co-channel cells by a minimum distance (D) to ensure RF isolation.

- **Reuse Ratio**: The trade-off between capacity and interference is defined by the co-channel reuse ratio $Q = D/R = \sqrt{3N}$, where R is the cell radius and N is the cluster size. A smaller cluster size N maximizes frequency reuse and system capacity, but a larger N is often required to improve the Signal-to-Interference Ratio (S/I) and transmission quality.

## 2. Adjacent Channel Interference

This type of interference is produced by signals leaking from channels that are adjacent in the frequency spectrum.

- **The Near-Far Problem**: It occurs due to the inability of mobile phone receiver filters to perfectly reject nearby frequencies. A major consequence is the near-far problem, which happens when a strong signal from a nearby transmitter overpowers and "captures" the receiver, completely blocking the weaker signal from the desired distant base station.

- **Mitigation**: To combat adjacent channel interference, systems rely on three key strategies: implementing highly accurate filtering, maximizing the frequency separation between channels assigned to the same cell, and utilizing strict power control so mobiles transmit at the lowest power necessary to maintain a link.

## 3. Trunking

Trunking is the foundational concept that allows a very large number of mobile users to share a much smaller number of available radio channels within a cell.

- Instead of permanently dedicating a channel to a specific user, channels are assigned strictly on a demand basis from a central pool.
- Once a user's call is completed, the assigned channel is immediately returned to the pool so it can be used by the next caller.
- The traffic intensity generated by users in a trunked system is measured in a unit called **Erlangs**.

## 4. Grade of Service (GoS)

The Grade of Service is the standard benchmark used to measure the performance and congestion of a trunked system, specifically evaluating a user's ability to access the network during the busiest hour of the day (statistically around 4 PM to 6 PM).

- It is defined as the probability that a call will be blocked or delayed beyond an acceptable time limit because all channels are currently in use.
- For example, if a cellular system is designed with a GoS of 2% blocking status, it guarantees that only 2 out of every 100 calls attempted during the busiest hour will be blocked.
- Estimating the required maximum capacity to meet a target GoS is a critical step in allocating the right number of radio channels during system design.
